Graduate Thesis Research

Evaluating Environmental and Psychological Variables in Collegiate Athletics


Data Analytics • Statistical Modeling • Research Design • PCA • Data Visualization

Project Overview

This graduate thesis explored how environmental and psychological variables influence collegiate athletic performance and whether these factors can improve predictive sports analytics models beyond traditional statistics alone.

The project involved survey development, data collection from collegiate athletes, statistical analysis in R, principal component analysis (PCA), correlation analysis, and interpretation of athlete performance factors.

Research Questions

  • How do environmental factors affect predictive accuracy in collegiate sports?
  • How do psychological variables influence athletic performance?
  • How can these variables be integrated into predictive models?

Methodology

  • Designed and distributed a survey to collegiate athletes
  • Collected quantitative athlete performance perception data
  • Performed statistical analysis using R
  • Applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
  • Conducted correlation analysis
  • Reviewed existing sports analytics literature

Key Findings

Travel Fatigue

34.1% of surveyed athletes identified travel fatigue as the single most impactful external factor affecting athletic performance.

Weather Conditions

Weather was consistently identified as a major performance factor, with cold temperatures receiving the highest number of responses.

Predictive Limitations

56.8% of athletes indicated that traditional statistics alone cannot fully predict game-day performance, supporting the inclusion of additional variables.
